Introduction¶

             Diabetes is a chronic condition that is striking Americans in ever-growing numbers, as the number of adults diagnosed with diabetes has doubled in the past twenty years.  Diabetes is a dangerous disease in itself, as skyrocketing and plummeting blood glucose levels can lead to ketoacidosis or coma, causing sudden death. More common than sudden death, though, are the diabetic complications that lead to prolonged morbidity and impaired quality of life.   
            The doubling of the rate of diabetes in the past 20 years is related to the increase in autoimmune diabetes (Type I), but more dramatically in diabetes related to obesity (Type II).  According to the CDC, approximately 10% of American adults have Type II diabetes, with an estimated 20% of these unaware of their condition. Diabetes is the 7th leading cause of mortality in the United States, and a profound cause of morbidity, as the leading cause of kidney failure, lower limb amputation, and adult blindness.  It is imperative to maintain controlled blood glucose to prevent these complications, both for patient quality of life and for economic reasons. Without proper care, the diabetic patient can rapidly develop a set of symptoms requiring advanced care that is both traumatic and expensive. Therefore, it is essential to determine what patients are most at risk of impending problems so that they can be treated proactively to prevent undue complications.
            The object of this project is to examine a selection of observable elements recorded during hospitalization of diabetic patients to determine which factors are most predictive of readmission. This would enable healthcare workers and the patients themselves to seek ongoing treatment that would prevent the need for hospital readmission.  
            The dataset used included fifty factors, none of which was a smoking gun, but many of which, taken together, suggested patients who were likely to be readmitted.

Data collection¶

A csv file obtained from Kaggle.com was used as a data source (https://www.kaggle.com/brandao/diabetes). The data was generally well-formulated but included string-based data which can cause future challenges. The given data included fifty observable attributes that could be predictive of readmission. This data includes demographic information, types of hospital admission, procedures performed during hospitalization, maximum and 3-month average glucose levels, insulin dose, 23 specific medication combinations that are mutually exclusive, change in status, and the target of this investigation, readmission. Data Wrangling¶

This data was fairly well-formatted, but it included many strings that required reformatting to be useful to me. I removed variables that showed the same result for all patients because they would be inherently useless as a predictor if both readmitted and non-readmitted patients had the same value.

Hypothesis Testing¶

An important part of the data science pipeline is hypothesis testing. This is used to determine if there is statistical significance. Two hypotheses are tested: the null hypothesis and the alternative hypothesis. If the value that the test produces, in the case the T value, is lower than the alpha value, the null hypothesis can be rejected and a statistically significant relationship can be shown, although this does not necessarily demonstrate causation. For more information, use the provided wikipedia link (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_hypothesis_testing).

Conclusions¶

Diabetes, both Type I and Type II, is presenting a growing problem for American patients and American healthcare. While acute events such as diabetic coma or ketoacidosis can cause sudden death, the primary health implications of diabetes are related to long-term poor blood glucose control which leads to complications in a variety of body systems. Being able to identify patients most likely to be experiencing this poor blood glucose control would enable the patients themselves and healthcare workers to treat this condition more aggressively. Patients requiring readmission are necessarily the group of patients most likely to be suffering poor glucose control. A data set from kaggle was imported as a csv file. While fairly well-formatted, there were many strings that required reformatting to be useful. Additionally, columns that contained the same value throughout were removed. Using machine learning to predict which observable factors were most likely to predict readmission, an algorithm was created to predict readmission for future patients. Additionally, columns that contained the same value throughout were removed, as they did not assist in prediction of readmission. While no factors were very predictive of readmission when taken independently, machine learning was able to classify patients into one of the three target categories: readmission in thirty days or less, readmission in greater than thirty days, or no readmission at all.
